01

💬 Communicative Language Teaching

Meaningful Practice

Communicative Language Teaching (CLT) (Canale & Swain, 1980; Widdowson, 1978) holds that language is best learned through authentic, meaning-focused use rather than isolated drills. Learners develop communicative competence by using English for a genuine purpose — describing their own lives, negotiating meaning, and producing personalised output.

My Perfect Week (Writing & Poem)

Pupils personalise a model poem about their weekly routine and share it aloud — language is tied to real, self-relevant content rather than abstract grammar practice, the hallmark of CLT's focus on meaning over form.

Role Play

Pupils simulate real-world exchanges (e.g. ordering at a restaurant, as a waiter) practising functional language and pragmatic competence — the classic CLT technique for building fluency and negotiation-of-meaning skills in a low-stakes context.

🏃 Total Physical Response

TPR

Asher's Total Physical Response (1969) links language input directly to physical action. Commands are understood and internalised through movement before learners are asked to produce speech, which lowers the affective filter (Krashen) and builds durable motor-linked memory for vocabulary and structures.

TPR Command Activities

Pupils physically execute commands given in English (e.g. action verbs) — the direct application of Asher's method.

Chant

Pupils perform different chants tied to different Superminds topics, pairing a physical mime with a rhythmic chant — movement plus rhythm doubly encodes the target vocabulary, consistent with TPR and the rhythmic/musical strand of Multiple Intelligences theory (Gardner, 1983).

03

🧩 Multisensory Learning

Constructivist Theory

Constructivism (Piaget, 1952) holds that learners build understanding by actively manipulating materials and discovering patterns themselves, rather than receiving facts passively. Multisensory instruction strengthens this by engaging visual, tactile and spatial channels simultaneously — especially effective for young or mixed-ability learners.

Tangrams

Pupils assemble shapes into pictures while naming/describing them, building spatial reasoning and vocabulary through hands-on discovery.

Skeletons

A hands-on assembly task (e.g. body-part vocabulary) where pupils construct meaning physically rather than by rote memorisation.

Picture Puzzle

Piecing together a picture engages visual-tactile problem-solving, and the completed image anchors new vocabulary in a concrete, self-discovered context.

04

🎮 Gamification

Game-Based Learning

Game-Based Learning theory and Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985) explain that points, competition, timers and instant feedback satisfy learners' needs for competence and autonomy, raising intrinsic motivation and time-on-task for repetitive practice that might otherwise feel like a drill.

Kahoot

Live, timed multiple-choice quizzes turn recall practice into a competitive class game.

Blooket

A game-show style quiz platform layering game mechanics onto vocabulary/grammar review.

TiQ-TaQ-ToQ

A tic-tac-toe style quiz (e.g. clothing vocabulary, “this/these”) — game mechanics layered over a grammar drill.

Word Search

A self-checking puzzle format that gamifies vocabulary/spelling recognition and rewards persistence.

Anagram

Unscrambling letters turns spelling practice into a game with a clear win-condition, increasing engagement with word-form recognition.

05

🤝 Cooperative Learning

Social Constructivism

Vygotsky's Social Constructivism (1978) and Johnson & Johnson's Cooperative Learning model propose that knowledge is built through social interaction: peers scaffold each other inside their Zone of Proximal Development, and structured group accountability leads to deeper processing than solo work.

Group Work

Pupils are organised into accountable groups (tracked via ClassDojo) to complete reading/grammar tasks together, distributing cognitive load and letting stronger peers model language for others.

06

🎧 Input-Based Learning

Krashen's Input Hypothesis / Audio-Lingual

Krashen's Input Hypothesis (1985) argues acquisition happens through comprehensible input slightly beyond current ability (“i+1”). The Audio-Lingual Method complements this by drilling fixed sentence patterns until automatic, using repetition and modelling rather than explicit rule explanation.

Reading Audio Book

Picture-supported vocabulary (fruit, school objects) is heard and repeated, giving pupils comprehensible, structured input plus pattern-drill practice.

07

🤖 AI-Assisted Learning

Scaffolding / Vygotsky's ZPD

Scaffolding (Vygotsky, 1978; Bruner, 1976) describes temporary support from a more knowledgeable other that is gradually withdrawn as the learner internalises a skill. Using AI as a writing model situates this within the Process Writing Approach, where drafting is supported before independent production.

ChatGPT — “My Favourite Season”

Pupils prompt AI for a model 80-word essay, study its structure and language, then reproduce/personalise it — the AI acts as a scaffold that models a text pupils could not yet produce unaided.

08

📖 Phonics-Based Instruction

Structural Approach

The Structural/Phonics approach teaches systematic sound-symbol correspondences and recurring word patterns (word families) so learners can decode and spell new words by analogy, building literacy bottom-up from sub-word units.

Word Family Build Book

The teacher gives pupils a ready-made book built around shared rimes (e.g. -at, -an, -ig) for them to use during practice, reinforcing phonemic pattern recognition and transferable decoding skills.
